Let's talk video games.

I am a pretty avid gamer. I've played loads of genres from FPS games to RPGs to platformers. I've always had a game or two ongoing at any point in my life. As such I've played a lot of good games and a lot of really average games. However, recently in the last few years I gained the ability to buy games with my own money due to the odd stage in life called college. This new found freedom and my innate inability to spend more than a couple hundred rupees at a time led me to indie games. I've played a few indie games before like Terraria and Minecraft but it was purely due to their popularity and less out of interest or enjoyment. 

They were good games to play with others and Terraria did leave a lasting impact on me but they were not the same as the Indie games I found later in life. My first Indie game was Hyper Light Drifter. An incredible art style combined with one of the most unique and incredible soundtracks blew me away. I had bought the game for hardly 500 rupees and I was blown away. It left me with an odd sense of guilt because I knew that the game was worth way more than that. I ended up getting curious and reading into the game's story and I found something that was truly beautiful. I recommend you go and look up the story of Alx Preston and how Hyper Light Drifter came to be. It made me realize that games are also a medium of art and worked by the same rules as other art forms. The more of your heart and soul you pour into something the more special it becomes.

I played a series of games since then such as Papers Please, Oxygen Not Included, Celeste, Hollow Knight (yet to finish this one, the Traitor Lord is brutal), Journey, Firewatch and so on. All these games were incredible and made me realize that a developer's creativity was not at all limited by their funding. However, they all lacked something crucial. It wasn't that they were bad or incomplete. I don't think there is anything that can be done to improve these games. They are in some sense "perfect". I never even realized what it was that these games lacked till I played Citizen Sleeper.

Now we get to Citizen Sleeper.

I bought the game on sale along with another game by the developer because the bundle was cheap. I didn't read the store page and didn't really look at reviews. I just bought it and saw reviews that it was a good game to play between work so I got intrigued and booted it up. Immediately I realized that it wasn't like any of the games I had played before. I had played games like XCOM that were turn based strategies that relied on luck but I never played a game with a table top dice system. Since I had never played any TTRPGs the experience was fresh in the beginning and kept me playing. 

Then I started reading the dialogue in Citizen Sleeper. That's when I realized I had stumbled onto a masterpiece unlike any other game I'd ever played before.

I am a reader by heart. I used to love novels and used to read a few books every month. At some point I lost the habit, it's a relatively common story in today's world. There were too many things fighting for my attention and I couldn't really pick up a book anymore but my love for them remained. Citizen Sleeper's dialogue and story writing brought back every single thing I missed in books and gave me an experience that I had never had in any of the hundreds of books I had read upto then.

I will attempt to explain what it is about the game that felt special. Citizen sleeper is (without too many spoilers) a game about the main character (which you play as) struggling to make a life in a space station called the Eye. It's a cyberpunk dystopia which is an extremely depressing genre in general. In fact in most of the cyberpunk media I have seen the common trend is that there are no happy endings. The cold blooded efficiency of coporations usually leaves no space for a happy life. The most incredible aspect of Citizen Sleeper's story is how they manage to write a story of hope and happiness in such a bleak hell. 

The story really hammers home the idea that happiness comes from small things. It builds an extemely human world which would be an incredible feat on its own, however, its more incredible when you realize that you aren't exactly human. Neither are many characters you meet in the story. This is the second most incredible part of this game. Its portrayal of non-human sapience is a masterpiece and in my opinion the best I've ever seen. In most media there is a tendency to humanize non-human creatures that are sapient. They are essentially just humans in a different body. They may have slightly different world views but nothing a human in their position wouldn't. 

This is what makes Citizen Sleeper unique.

The artificial life in this game feel distinctly alien. They are clearly alive and thinking beings but they do not feel human. They are different from us and have a different way of looking at the world that is entirely alien to us as humans. They feel less like humans and more like gods, but unlike gods they are not all powerful. There are beings that are as mortal as you and there are beings that you cannot comprehend at all. 

However, the most striking part of this game was something I only realized when I was nearly done with the game and I was talking about it with my sister who was also playing the game. I realized that at some point I stopped seeing the game as a game. It was a game that gave you choices that impacted the story and at some point the choices I could make were fixed but not by game design but by my own personality. The game felt so real that I couldn't imagine picking any other choice. It was the first game where the difficult choices didn't leave me wanting to go back and see where the other option led. It was so well written that I couldn't bring myself to play god with the characters like I had in every other game. My sense of a fourth wall was severely damaged.
